 342  <h1>RESEARCH WITH THE LECTICAL ASSESSMENT SYSTEM (LAS)</h1>
 343      <div align="center">
 344                  <p>Test your developmental intuition: Take the <a href="../sorttask/sorttasklead.php">Sort Task Challenge</a> </p>

 346                <p>
 347                At<strong> DTS </strong>we have an active research program that keeps us on the leading edge of assessment and curriculum design. We work with academic, corporate, and government clients to understand, describe, and promote learning in a range of knowledge domains.</p>
 348                <p>We employ the<span class="emphasis"> Lectical Assessment System</span> (<a href="../PDF/LAS.pdf">LAS</a>)—and a set of research methods—<em><a href="../_about/devmaieutics.php">developmental maieutics</a></em>—to study, describe, and assess the development of reasoning in many areas of knowledge. </p>
 349                <p>On this page, we provide brief descriptions of our methods and research. If you want more in-depth information, see our <a href="../_about/articles.php">articles</a> and <a href="../projects/projects_landing_page.php">projects</a> pages. You can also visit <a href="ttp://discotest.org">DiscoTest</a> to see how we are using our methods to change classroom testing.<br />

 353          <div class="AccordionPanelTab">Working with the LAS</div>
 354                      <div class="AccordionPanelContent">
 355                        <p>Historically, if you wanted to describe how a skill or concept developed, you would have tested  a group of volunteers several times over many years. That way, you'd gradually build up an understanding of how their knowledge changed over time. This process was effective and accurate, but costly—in both time and money. Consequently, only a few learning sequences have been described in this way.                      </p>
 356                        <p>That's where Theo Dawson came in. She wanted to make developmental assessment practical and affordable. Fortunately, <a href="http://www.piaget.org/">Piaget</a>, <a href="http://www.gse.harvard.edu/~ddl/aboutkwf.htm">Fischer</a>, <a href="http://www.tiac.net/%7ecommons/dare_institute.html">Commons</a> and a few other developmental researchers had already provided general descriptions of the developmental spiral. Dawson took this existing knowledge and built upon it to devise the Lectical™ Assessment System (<span class="emphasis"><a href="../PDF/LAS.pdf">LAS</a></span>). The levels and tiers that comprise the lectical scale were first described by <a href="http://gseweb.harvard.edu/%7eddl/articlesCopy/FischerTheoryCognDev1980W.pdf">Fischer</a>.</p>
 357                        <p>She then designed an effective <a href="http://lectica.info/scoring/conceptanalysis.php">new methodology</a> for describing learning pathways. Once pathways have been described, it becomes possible to create <span class="emphasis">(1)</span> high quality developmental assessments, <span class="style1">(2)</span> scoring rubrics, and <span class="emphasis">(3)</span> developmentally informed curricula.      </p>

 397                      <div class="AccordionPanelTab">What makes the LAS so special?</div>
 398                      <div class="AccordionPanelContent">
 399                        <p>The <a href="../PDF/LAS.pdf">LAS</a> makes it possible to describe learning sequences without conducting long-term studies of learning. This is because, unlike <a href="scoring_comparison.html">other developmental assessment systems</a>, it functions as a developmental <a href="../_about/measurement.php">ruler</a>. With it, we can score any performance in any area of knowledge. No matter what the subject matter is, we can use the ruler to study it. Although the lectical scale has 13 levels, our assessments only target the top 5—the levels  most commonly observed in adolescence and adulthood. </p>
 400                        <p>We can do many things with the LAS that can’t be done with other measures. We have used the LAS to:<br />
 401                        </p>
 402                        <ul>
 403                          <li>describe how adults learn essential skills;<br />
 404                          </li>
 405                          <li>assess the thinking skills of employees;<br />
 406                          </li>
 407                          <li>analyze the task demands of jobs;<br />
 408                          </li>
 409                          <li>describe knowledge and skill standards for management positions;<br />
 410                          </li>
 411                          <li>determine appropriate learning objectives for individuals; <br />
 412                          </li>
 413                          <li>evaluate curricula designed to improve thinking skills; and<br />
 414                          </li>
 415                          <li>design customized learning recommendations.                    </li>
 416                        </ul>

 420                      <div class="AccordionPanelTab">How we use the LAS to determine how people learn</div>
 421                      <div class="AccordionPanelContent">
 422                        <p>To explain how we use the <a href="../PDF/LAS.pdf">LAS</a> to describe learning sequences, we'll use an example from our leadership work. First, we interviewed about 300 people between the ages of 5 and 65 about their conceptions of good leadership. Then, our lectical analysts scored the interviews for their lectical level. At the same time, another group of researchers coded the conceptual content of the interviews. Once the scoring and coding were finished, we were able to examine the relation between <em>what</em> people said and the lectical <em>levels</em> of their performances. This made it possible to describe what leadership reasoning looked like at each level represented in our data.</p>
 423                        <p>We then went on to analyze the task demands of different management levels, assigning them to phases of development. As part of this process, we described a variety of leadership <a href="../PDF/IntegratedAction.pdf"> </a>competency requirements for 5 management levels. These competency sequences parallel the developmental sequences for acquiring these skills. Within the next few years, we expect  to complete the first exhaustive account of the development of central leadership skills. We invite you to view a summary of the sequence for a competency one of our customers calls <a href="../PDF/IntegratedAction.pdf">integrated action</a>.                    </p>

 445                      <div class="AccordionPanelTab">Improving instruction</div>
 446                      <div class="AccordionPanelContent">
 447                        <p>We use what we discover about  learning pathways to devise and test curricula. In conventional approaches to curriculum development, content experts work together to determine the sequence in which material should be learned. At DTS, we not only rely on the knowledge of content experts, we study learning to determine how concepts are actually acquired. The approach is called <a href="../_about/devmaieutics.php"><em>developmental maieutics</em></a>. </p>
 448                        <p>By combining the knowledge of content experts with knowledge gleaned from research on conceptual development, we can identify the learning tasks and  optimal learning pathways for any knowledge domain.                    </p>

 452                      <div class="AccordionPanelTab">Our approach to curriculum development</div>
 453                      <div class="AccordionPanelContent">
 454                        <p>Our approach to curriculum development involves several steps:</p>
 455                        <ol>
 456                          <li>Working with clients to identify an appropriate base of expert knowledge in a given domain. For example, the Decision Making Database, a database designed to inform a three-level decision making curriculum for government managers, is based on a developmental analysis of a set of decision making texts selected by our client with the assistance of several decision making experts.</li>
 457                          <li>Employing the expert knowledge base identified in step one to identify the skills that make up a given domain. These skills are presented as a hierarchically organized <a href="skill_map.html">skill map</a>.</li>
 458                          <li>Defining the developmental sequence for building each of the skills identified in step 2.</li>
 459                          <li>Identifying the “teaching points” addressed in the base of expert knowledge identified in step 1, and assessing the developmental level of the knowledge presented in each of these teaching points.</li>
 460                          <li>Identifying the skills (from step 2) represented in each of the teaching points identified in step 4.                    </li>
 461                        </ol>

 465                      <div class="AccordionPanelTab">Program evaluation</div>
