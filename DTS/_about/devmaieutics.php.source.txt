<h1>DEVELOPMENTAL MAIEUTICS</h1>
                <p>The best assessments are grounded in a deep understanding of how relevant concepts and skills develop over time. For example, if a curriculum is intended to develop decision-making skills, assessments should evaluate developmental progress in acquiring those skills. To do this well, it is essential to meet 3 objectives:</p>
                <ul>
                  <li>identifying the range of skills required for good decision-making various life or job roles; </li>
                  <li>tracing the developmental pathways through which decision-making skills optimally develop; and </li>
                  <li>identifying effective strategies for promoting the development of these skills. </li>
                </ul>
                <p align="center"><img src="../_images/methods/maieutic_spiral.png" alt="maieutic spiral" class="margin-t20-b20" style="max-width:780px; width:100%; "/></p>
              <p>We have designed an iterative methodology that is employed to build the knowledge base required to meet these objectives. We call this methodology&mdash;represented in the spirals shown above&mdash;<em>developmental maieutics</em>. It involves several steps:</p>
              <ol>
                  <li>Establishing a collaborative relationship with clients (researchers, end users, and content experts).</li>
                  <li>Leveraging the expertise of clients to identify assessment goals and target constructs. </li>
                <li>Working closely with  clients and  domain experts to design a pool of items and collect initial student data.</li>
                <li>Scoring performances with the Lectical Assessment System,  and entering the first phase of the learning-sequence-construction process, which involves identifying and codifying themes (broad thematic areas, eg., <em>evidence, the physics of energy</em>), constructs (sub-themes, eg., <em>evaluating evidence, energy transformation</em>), and judgment strands (sub-constructs, e.g., <em>credibility</em>, <em>persuasion as argumentation, kinetic energy</em>) and describing student judgments at each developmental phase on each strand.</li>
                <li>Organizing the results of D into scoring rubrics for three levels of coder expertise: (1) <em>low inference</em> (for novices), in which coding is done at the judgment strand level, (2) <em>medium inference</em> (for junior coders), in which coding is done at the construct level, and (3) <em>high inference</em> (for senior coders), in which coding is done at the theme level. Also, as analysts develop rubrics, curriculum experts  can begin developing complementary curricular materials and report content.</li>
                <li>Releasing Teasers to teachers and researchers.</li>
                <li>Iterating continuously based on  classroom data and feedback from users.</li>
              </ol>
              <p>With adequate funding, a fully developed set (2-4 alternate forms of the same assessment) of Teasers, complete with curricular materials, learning resources, and reports, can be developed in about 3 years. During the development phase, before Teasers are ready to be released to the teachers of the world, they are frequently used in curriculum evaluations conducted by researchers and schools.</p>
              <p>To learn more about skill levels, see the <a href="hierarchical_complexity.php">hierarchical complexity</a> and <a href="skill_levels.php">skill level</a> pages.</p>
