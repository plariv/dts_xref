 140  <h1>Skill levels    </h1>
 141  <p>The following table provides descriptions of skill levels 6-12. If you'd like to learn more about the principles behind the Lectical Assessment System, see our <a href="hierarchical_complexity.php">hierarchical complexity</a> page.</p>
 142          <table width="96%" class="minimalist-a margin-t20-b20">
 143                <tr>
 144                  <th width="15%">Level</th>
 145                  <th width="45%">Concepts</th>
 146                  <th>Logical structure</th>
 147                </tr>
 148                <tr>
 149                  <td><strong>6</strong><br />
 150                    <br />
 151                    <strong>Single representations<br />
 152                      26-40 mos</strong></td>
 153                  <td><strong>Concepts are 1st order representational sets</strong><br />
 154                    These coordinate symbolic systems. In responses to the Joe dilemma, for example, the concept of camping coordinates activities like swimming, sleeping in a tent, and painting, and the concept of a paper route coordinates activities like riding a bike, delivering papers, and receiving money.</td>
 155                  <td><strong>The logical structure is definitional</strong><br />
 156                    It identifies one aspect of a single representation—as in &quot;Camping is fun,&quot; in which fun is an &quot;aspect&quot; of camp.</td>
 157                </tr>
 158                <tr>
 159                  <td><strong>7</strong><br />
 160                    <br />
 161                    <strong>Representational mappings<br />
 162                      4-5 years</strong></td>
 163                  <td><strong>Concepts are 2nd order representational sets</strong><br />
 164                    These coordinate or modify representational sets (the concepts constructed at the single representations level). The very popular representational mappings Lectical™ level concept of having favorites, for example, can be employed to rank camping and fishing. &quot;Camping is my favorite, and fishing is my next favorite.&quot; Concepts like <i>being mean, keeping a promise, changing one’s mind</i>, and <i>sharing</i> also become common at this Lectical™ level. &quot;[Joe’s father] is just being mean; he is taking the money away from his kids.&quot;</td>
 165                  <td><strong>The logical structure is linear</strong><br />
 166                    It coordinates one aspect of two or more representations—as in, &quot;If you do not do what your father tells you to do, he will get really mad at you,&quot; in which doing what your father says and not doing what your father says are coordinated by his anticipated reaction.</td>
 167                </tr>
 168                <tr>
 169                  <td><strong>8</strong><br />
 170                    <br />
 171                    <strong>Representational systems<br />
 172                      6-7 years</strong></td>
 173                  <td><strong>Concepts are 3rd order representational sets</strong><br />
 174                    These coordinate elements of representational systems. For example, the concept of trust, articulated for the first time at this Lectical™ level, can be used to describe the system of interactions between Joe and his father. &quot;Joe trusted [his Dad] that he could go to the camp if he saved enough money, and then his father just breaks it, and the promise is very important.&quot; Concepts like <i>to turn against, to blame, to believe</i>, and <i>being fair</i> are also infrequently observed before this level. &quot;[If you break a promise] they will not like you anymore, and your friends will turn against you.&quot;</td>
 175                  <td><strong>The logical structure is multivariate</strong><br />
 176                    It coordinates multiple aspects of two or more representations—as in, &quot;If Joe’s Dad says Joe can go to camp, then he says he can’t go to camp, that’s not fair because Joe worked hard and then his Dad changed his mind,&quot; in which two conflicting representations of Dad’s authority are evaluated in terms of his changed mind and Joe’s hard work.</td>
 177                </tr>
 178                <tr>
 179                  <td><strong>9</strong><br />
 180                    <strong><br />
 181                      Single abstractions<br />
 182                      9-11 years</strong></td>
 183                  <td><strong>Concepts are 1st order abstractions</strong><br />
 184                    These coordinate representational systems. For example, the concept of trustworthiness, articulated for the first time at this Lectical™ level, defines those qualities that make a person trustworthy rather than describing a particular situation in which trust is felt or not felt. It is composed of qualities that produce trust, such as <i>telling the truth, keeping secrets</i>, and<i> keeping promises</i>. &quot;It's always nice…to be trustworthy. Because, then, if [someone has] a secret, they can come and talk to you.&quot; Concepts like<i> kindness, keeping your word, respect</i>, and <i>guilt</i> are also rare before the this level. &quot;If you don't do something you promise, you'll feel really guilty.&quot;</td>
 185                  <td><strong>The logical structure is definitional</strong><br />
 186                    It identifies one aspect of a single abstraction—as in, &quot;Making a promise is giving your word,&quot; in which giving one's word is an &quot;aspect&quot; of a promise.</td>
 187                </tr>
 188                <tr>
 189                  <td><strong>10<br />
 190                    <br />
 191                    Abstract mappings</strong></td>
 192                  <td><strong>Concepts are 2nd order abstractions </strong><br />
 193                    These coordinate or modify abstractions. For example, the abstract mappings level concept basis can be employed to coordinate the elements essential to a good relationship. &quot;To me, [trust and respect are] the basis of a relationship, and without them you really don't have one.&quot; Concepts like <i>coming to an agreement, making a commitment, building trust</i>, and <i>compromise</i> are also rare before this Lectical™ level. &quot;I think [Joe and his father] could come to an agreement or compromise that they are both comfortable with.&quot;</td>
 194                  <td><strong>The logical structure is linear</strong><br />
 195                    The most complex logical structure of this Lectical™ level coordinates one aspect of two or more abstractions—as in, &quot;Joe has a right to go to camp because his father said he could go if he saved up the money, and Joe lived up to his commitment.&quot; Here, Joe's fulfillment of his father's conditions determines whether Joe has a right or does not have a right to go to camp.</td>
 196                </tr>
 197                <tr>
 198                  <td><strong>11<br />
 199                    <br />
 200                    Abstract systems</strong></td>
 201                  <td><strong>Concepts are 3rd order abstractions</strong><br />
 202                    These coordinate elements of abstract systems. For example, the concept of personal integrity–which is rare before the abstract systems level–refers to the coordination of and adherence to notions of fairness, trustworthiness, honesty, preservation of the golden rule, etc., in one’s actions. &quot;[You should keep your word] for your own integrity. For your own self-worth, really. Just to always be the kind of person that you would want to be dealing with.&quot; Concepts like <i>verbal contract, moral commitment, functional, development, social structure</i>, and <i>foundation</i> are also uncommon before the abstract systems level. &quot;A promise is the verbal contract, the moral commitment that the father made to his son. It is the only way for the child to…develop his moral thinking—from watching his parent's moral attitude.&quot;</td>
 203                  <td><strong>The logical structure is multivariate</strong><br />
 204                    The most complex logical structure of this level coordinates multiple aspects of two or more abstractions. &quot;Following through with his commitment and actually experiencing camp combine to promote Joe’s growth and development, not just physically but psychologically, emotionally, and spiritually.&quot; Here, multiple facets of Joe’s personal development are promoted when he both keeps his commitment and accomplishes his goal.</td>
 205                </tr>
 206                <tr>
 207                  <td><strong>12</strong><br />
 208                    <br />
 209                    <strong>Single axioms/ principles</strong></td>
 210                  <td><strong>Concepts are 1st order axioms/ principles</strong><br />
 211                    These coordinate abstract systems. The notion of the social contract, for example, results from the coordination of human interests (where individual human beings are treated as systems). &quot;Everybody wants to be treated equally and have a sense of fair play. Because this is so, we have an obligation to one another to enter into a social contract that optimizes equality and fairness.&quot; Concepts like <em><i>autonomy, fair play, heteronomy, higher order principle</i>,</em> and <i><em>philosophical principle</em></i> are rare before the single axioms/ principles level. &quot;The only time we’re justified in breaking the social contract is when a higher principle, such as the right to life, intervenes.&quot;</td>
 212                  <td><strong>The logical structure is definitional</strong><br />
 213                    It identifies one aspect of a principle or axiom coordinating systems—as in, &quot;Contracts are articulations of a unique human quality, mutual trust, which coordinates human relations.&quot; Here, contracts are seen as the instantiation of a broader principle coordinating human interactions.</td>
 214                </tr>

 216              <h3>Scoring</h3>
 217              <p>Learning to score with the LAS is a long and challenging process that involves many hours of instruction and study. At this time, we only teach  qualified individuals who are working with DTS/DiscoTest. Our Certified Analysts are the only people who can legally call themselves Lectical Analysts or claim to be using the LAS to score performances.</p>
 218              <p>We also offer an annual course, Foundations of Lectical Assessment (<a href="../courses/about_course.php?coursekey=102">FOLA</a>), that covers the core literature and basics of the scoring system. The FOLA does not qualify participants to score, but does provide the basic knowledge required to effectively work with or conduct research with LectaTests and DiscoTests.</p>
