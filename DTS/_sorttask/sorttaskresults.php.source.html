 164          <h1>What we've learned  from the Sort Task Challenge</h1>
 165                  <p><img src="../images/4-45LevelsStars.gif" alt="Developmental Ruler" width="86" height="626" align="right" />We've been asking people to do the sort task for several years, and we've documented some interesting trends, but before we tell you about them, we'd better let you in on a couple of secrets. </p>
 166                  <ol>
 167                    <li>
 168                      The statements you sorted were not really made by one person. They were made by several different people. No one has ever studied the cognitive development of a single person for 41 years.
 169                    </li>
 170                    <li>
 171                    Not all of the statements were at different developmental levels. Only 7 distinct levels were represented in the statements (6-12). So, if you found it very difficult to order some of the statements, it's because they weren't as different from one another as some of the other statements. However, even though some pairs of statements were at the same developmental level, they were still different because they were at different phases of the same level. The figure on the right shows how the statements were distributed along our developmental metric. As you can see, some were much closer together than others. </li>
 172                  </ol>
 173                  <h3>Patterns in the way people do the sort task</h3>
 174                  <p>Most people come very close to getting the right order. In fact, so far, when we randomly choose any 10 adults from our current sample, the sort order selected by the majority of these people is always the correct order—up to a point. Many people accurately sort the statements into the 7 levels, but are likely to mix up the order of statements that are at different phases within a level.</p>
 175                  <p><span class="emphasis">Age and education:</span> Adolescents and less educated adults seem to have have more difficulty distinguishing between the level 11 and level 12 tasks than more educated adults. Other researchers suggest that this may be because they  reason at level 10 and therefore cannot distinguish between performances at the higher levels.</p>
 176                  <p><span class="emphasis">Profession:</span> Teachers and mathematicians appear to sort the cards more accurately than people in other professions. We don't find it particularly surprising that teachers do well. They need to understand how their students think in order to teach. But mathematicians!? Actually, it isn't as much of a mystery as you might think. It has to do with the criteria they use to do the sort. They are more likely than any other group to say they looked at the &quot;structure&quot; of each statement, and that's what we do when we score a performance.</p>
 177                  <h3>Criteria people use for sorting</h3>
 178                  <p>People provide a range of criteria for the sort orders they choose, including increases in:</p>
 179                  <ul>
 180                    <li>
 181                      the amount of knowledge apparent in a quote;
 182                    </li>
 183                    <li>
 184                      the &quot;abstractness&quot; of language;
 185                    </li>
 186                    <li>
 187                      the complexity of syntax or logic;
 188                    </li>
 189                    <li>
 190                      the number of perspectives taken into account;
 191                    </li>
 192                    <li>
 193                      amount of generalization or breadth;
 194                    </li>
 195                    <li>
 196                      the sophistication of the language or argument; and
 197                    </li>
 198                    <li>
 199                      how &quot;smart&quot; the person sounds.
