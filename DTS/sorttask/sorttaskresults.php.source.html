 540                  <h3>Patterns in the way people do the sort task</h3>
 541                  <p>Most people come very close to getting the right order. In fact, so far, when we randomly choose any 10 adults from our current sample, the sort order selected by the majority of these people is always the correct order—up to a point. Many people accurately sort the statements into the 7 levels, but are likely to mix up the order of statements that are at different phases of the same level.</p>
 542                  <p><span class="emphasis">Age and education:</span> Adolescents and less educated adults seem to have have more difficulty distinguishing between the level 11 and level 12 tasks than more educated adults. Other researchers suggest that this may be because they  reason at level 10 and therefore cannot distinguish between performances at the higher levels.</p>
 543                  <p><span class="emphasis">Profession:</span> Teachers and mathematicians appear to sort the cards more accurately than people in other professions. We don't find it particularly surprising that teachers do well. They need to understand how their students think in order to teach. But mathematicians!? Actually, it isn't as much of a mystery as you might think. It has to do with the criteria they use to do the sort. They are more likely than any other group to say they looked at the &quot;structure&quot; of each statement, and that's what we do when we score a performance.</p>
