 762                    <h3 align="left"><span>Implicit versus explicit knowledge</span></h3>
 763                    <p align="left"><span>Some of our knowlege is readily avaialble to us. We are conscious of it and can share it with others. Psychologists refer to this kind of knowledge as <em>explicit</em>. Explicit knowledge is fairly easy to share on an assessment like this one. However, people often know more about a particular subject than they can communicate in an assessment like this one. This is because much of what we learn through experience does not automatically become part of our explicit knowledge. It's in a form that's difficult to share. We call this kind of knowledge <em>tacit</em> or <em>implicit</em>. You can also think of it as intuitive knowledge. Tacit knowledge is not a bad thing. It helps us make quick choices in familiar situations; we'd be in big trouble if we had to think through every single situation in our lives before we made a decision! But tacit knowledge has its limits.</span>          </p>
 764  <p><strong>First</strong>, because we aren't able to bring it into focus, we can't share tacit knowledge with others. This is the case in businesses in which the person at the top is an &quot;intuitive&quot; leader. Because his or her leadership skills are tacit, they can't be shared. In situations like this, it is not uncommon for an institution to last only as long as its leader. </p>
 765                    <p><strong>Second</strong>, it is limited by our experience, which means it isn't terribly useful for dealing with novelty. Because tacit knowledge is experienced-based, it is most useful in situations that are like those we have confronted in the past. Unfortunately, the modern world is constantly providing us with new challenges. To meet these challenges, we  need conscious methods for evaluating  knowledge and experience.</p>
 766                    <p><strong>Third</strong>, sometimes our tacit knowledge is sub-optimal. For example, people who learn the skills required to survive on the rough side of town or in an abusive relationship often fare poorly when they try to function outside of those contexts, because their  tacit knowledge isn't useful in their new surroundings. Before they can learn more adaptive skills, they usually need to bring their tacit knowledge into consciousness. </p>
 767                    <p><span><strong>Fourth</strong>, relying on tacit knowledge limits our development. When we habitually rely on our tacit knowledge, learning not only slows down, it's quality is compromised. Optimal learning requires that we reflect consciously upon our own experiences and actively seek other perspectives to</span> fill in the gaps. </p>
 768                    <p><strong>Finally</strong>, relying upon tacit knowledge limits our ability to collaborate with others.</p>
