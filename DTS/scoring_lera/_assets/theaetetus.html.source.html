 435        Theaetetus
 436        By Plato    </h1>
 437      <h3>From the Internet Classics Archive    </h3>
 438      <p>Translated by Benjamin Jowett</p>
 439      <p>Persons of the Dialogue:
 440        SOCRATES,
 441        THEODORUS,
 442        THEAETETUS</p>
 443      <p>Scene:
 444        <em>Euclid</em> and Terpsion meet in front of <em>Euclid</em>'s house in Megara; they
 445        enter the house, and the dialogue is read to them by a servant.    </p>
 446      <p><em>Euclid</em>. Have you only just arrived from the country, Terpsion?</p>
 447      <p>Terpsion. No, I came some time ago: and I have been in the Agora looking
 448        for you, and wondering that I could not find you. </p>
 449      <p><em>Euclid.</em> But I was not in the city. </p>
 450      <p><em>Terp.</em> Where then? </p>
 451      <p><em>Euclid.</em> As I was going down to the harbour, I met Theaetetus-he was being
 452        carried up to Athens from the army at Corinth. </p>
 453      <p><em>Terp.</em> Was he alive or dead? </p>
 454      <p><em>Euclid.</em> He was scarcely alive, for he has been badly wounded; but he
 455        was suffering even more from the sickness which has broken out in
 456        the army. </p>
 457      <p><em>Terp.</em> The dysentery, you mean? </p>
 458      <p><em>Euclid.</em> Yes. </p>
 459      <p><em>Terp.</em> Alas! what a loss he will be! </p>
 460      <p><em>Euclid.</em> Yes, Terpsion, he is a noble fellow; only to-day I heard some
 461        people highly praising his behaviour in this very battle.</p>
 462      <p><em>Terp.</em> No wonder; I should rather be surprised at hearing anything
 463        else of him. But why did he go on, instead of stopping at Megara?</p>
 464      <p><em>Euclid.</em> He wanted to get home: although I entreated and advised him to
 465        remain he would not listen to me; so I set him on his way, and turned
 466        back, and then I remembered what Socrates had said of him, and thought
 467        how remarkably this, like all his predictions, had been fulfilled.
 468        I believe that he had seen him a little before his own death, when
 469        Theaetetus was a youth, and he had a memorable conversation with him,
 470        which he repeated to me when I came to Athens; he was full of admiration
 471        of his genius, and said that he would most certainly be a great man,
 472        if he lived. </p>
 473      <p><em>Terp.</em> The prophecy has certainly been fulfilled; but what was the
 474        conversation? can you tell me? </p>
 475      <p><em>Euclid.</em> No, indeed, not offhand; but I took notes of it as soon as I
 476        got home; these I filled up from memory, writing them out at leisure;
 477        and whenever I went to Athens, I asked Socrates about any point which
 478        I had forgotten, and on my return I made corrections; thus I have
 479        nearly the whole conversation written down. </p>
 480      <p><em>Terp.</em> I remember-you told me; and I have always been intending to
 481        ask you to show me the writing, but have put off doing so; and now,
 482        why should we not read it through?-having just come from the country,
 483        I should greatly like to rest. </p>
 484      <p><em>Euclid.</em> I too shall be very glad of a rest, for I went with Theaetetus
 485        as far as Erineum. Let us go in, then, and, while we are reposing,
 486        the servant shall read to us. </p>
 487      <p><em>Terp.</em> Very good. </p>
 488      <p><em>Euclid</em>. Here is the roll, Terpsion; I may observe that I have introduced
 489        Socrates, not as narrating to me, but as actually conversing with
 490        the persons whom he mentioned-these were, Theodorus the geometrician
 491        (of Cyrene), and Theaetetus. I have omitted, for the sake of convenience,
 492        the interlocutory words &quot;I said,&quot; &quot;I remarked,&quot; which he used when
 493        he spoke of himself, and again, &quot;he agreed,&quot; or &quot;disagreed,&quot; in the
 494        answer, lest the repetition of them should be troublesome.</p>
 495      <p><em>Terp.</em> Quite right, Euclid. </p>
 496      <p><em>Euclid</em>. And now, boy, you may take the roll and read. </p>
 497      <p><em>Euclid</em>'s servant reads. </p>
 498      <p><em>Socrates.</em> If I cared enough about the Cyrenians, Theodorus, I would
 499        ask you whether there are any rising geometricians or philosophers
 500        in that part of the world. But I am more interested in our own Athenian
 501        youth, and I would rather know who among them are likely to do well.
 502        I observe them as far as I can myself, and I enquire of any one whom
 503        they follow, and I see that a great many of them follow you, in which
 504        they are quite right, considering your eminence in geometry and in
 505        other ways. Tell me then, if you have met with any one who is good
 506        for anything. </p>
 507      <p><em>Theodorus.</em> Yes, Socrates, I have become acquainted with one very remarkable
 508        Athenian youth, whom I commend to you as well worthy of your attention.
 509        If he had been a beauty I should have been afraid to praise him, lest
 510        you should suppose that I was in love with him; but he is no beauty,
 511        and you must not be offended if I say that he is very like you; for
 512        he has a snub nose and projecting eyes, although these features are
 513        less marked in him than in you. Seeing, then, that he has no personal
 514        attractions, I may freely say, that in all my acquaintance, which
 515        is very large, I never knew anyone who was his equal in natural gifts:
 516        for he has a quickness of apprehension which is almost unrivalled,
 517        and he is exceedingly gentle, and also the most courageous of men;
 518        there is a union of qualities in him such as I have never seen in
 519        any other, and should scarcely have thought possible; for those who,
 520        like him, have quick and ready and retentive wits, have generally
 521        also quick tempers; they are ships without ballast, and go darting
 522        about, and are mad rather than courageous; and the steadier sort,
 523        when they have to face study, prove stupid and cannot remember. Whereas
 524        he moves surely and smoothly and successfully in the path of knowledge
 525        and enquiry; and he is full of gentleness, flowing on silently like
 526        a river of oil; at his age, it is wonderful. </p>
 527      <p><em>Socrates.</em> That is good news; whose son is he? </p>
 528      <p><em>Theodorus.</em> The name of his father I have forgotten, but the youth himself
 529        is the middle one of those who are approaching us; he and his companions
 530        have been anointing themselves in the outer court, and now they seem
 531        to have finished, and are towards us. Look and see whether you know
 532        him. </p>
 533      <p><em>Socrates.</em> I know the youth, but I do not know his name; he is the son of
 534        Euphronius the Sunian, who was himself an eminent man, and such another
 535        as his son is, according to your account of him; I believe that he
 536        left a considerable fortune. </p>
 537      <p><em>Theodorus.</em> Theaetetus, Socrates, is his name; but I rather think that
 538        the property disappeared in the hands of trustees; notwithstanding
 539        which he is wonderfully liberal. </p>
 540      <p><em>Socrates.</em> He must be a fine fellow; tell him to come and sit by me.</p>
