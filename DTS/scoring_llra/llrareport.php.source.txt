if (!function_exists("GetSQLValueString")) {
function GetSQLValueString($theValue, $theType, $theDefinedValue = "", $theNotDefinedValue = "") 
{
  if (PHP_VERSION < 6) {
    $theValue = get_magic_quotes_gpc() ? stripslashes($theValue) : $theValue;
  }

  $theValue = function_exists("mysql_real_escape_string") ? mysql_real_escape_string($theValue) : mysql_escape_string($theValue);

  switch ($theType) {
    case "text":
      $theValue = ($theValue != "") ? "'" . $theValue . "'" : "NULL";
      break;    
    case "long":
    case "int":
      $theValue = ($theValue != "") ? intval($theValue) : "NULL";
      break;
    case "double":
      $theValue = ($theValue != "") ? doubleval($theValue) : "NULL";
      break;
    case "date":
      $theValue = ($theValue != "") ? "'" . $theValue . "'" : "NULL";
      break;
    case "defined":
      $theValue = ($theValue != "") ? $theDefinedValue : $theNotDefinedValue;
      break;
  }
  return $theValue;
}
}

                <p>On this page we provide you with suggestions  designed to help you move  to the next phase in your development as a leader. These suggestions are based on extensive research into the sequences through which leaders commonly learn particular leadership skills. However, your own development  is likely to progress at somewhat different rates on different skills, and you may find that some  learning resources  seem more challenging than others. If you think you already have a good grasp of concepts presented in a given text, you are probably ready to move to the next level in the skill area represented in that text. If this happens, move to the next topic. We encourage you to work on your leadership skills with the support of a mentor who can provide feedback on your success in translating what you learn into action.</p>
                <p>As you work with the resources suggested on this page, keep in mind that deep learning requires practice. You are far more likely to incorporate new knowledge into your skill repertoire if you take every opportunity to (1) put it to work, and (2) reflect upon outcomes. Putting knowledge to work means applying it in real-world contexts. Try out new skills in low-stakes situations first, then gradually begin to apply them in higher-stakes situations until you've mastered them. Reflecting on outcomes means evaluating  the results of  your efforts. This works best if you keep a record—such as a journal—and share your experiences with other members of your community of practice. If you are a senior manager or CEO, you may want to think about how your corporate culture supports or prevents this kind of discourse.</p>

<p>In this simplified image, the task demands of most management positions increase in a linear fashion, spanning levels 10-13+. The capabilities of managers do not, for the most part, match these task demands. This means that the decision-making of average managers is likely to be less complex than many of the issues they confront. In concrete terms, this means failing to understand or take into account important aspects these issues, resulting in sub-optimal decision making.</p>
                <p>The disturbing pattern shown in the figure is pervasive—we see it everywhere we look—and it reflects a hard truth. Few, if any, of us are capable of meeting the task demands of the most complex situations in today’s world. We have concluded  that in many situations our best hope for meeting these demands is to (1) work strategically on the development of our own skills and knowledge, (2) learn to work closely with others who represent a wide range of perspectives and areas of expertise, and (3) use the best tools available to scaffold our thinking.</p>
                <p>The task demands of the highest levels of management/leadership in most large (and many smaller) institutions are far beyond the reach of individual leaders. At these levels, leaders must work with others to identify and integrate a wide range of knowledge, information, and perspectives. In these cases, the performance level of the group can be a full level higher than the performance levels of the individuals who make up the group. </p>
                <p>Because your personal growth and performance as a leader depend upon your capacity for working with others, we urge you to work diligently to improve your interpersonal skills, particularly those required for collaboration.</p>
