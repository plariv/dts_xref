function GetSQLValueString($theValue, $theType, $theDefinedValue = "", $theNotDefinedValue = "") 
{
  if (PHP_VERSION < 6) {
    $theValue = get_magic_quotes_gpc() ? stripslashes($theValue) : $theValue;
  }

  $theValue = function_exists("mysql_real_escape_string") ? mysql_real_escape_string($theValue) : mysql_escape_string($theValue);

  switch ($theType) {
    case "text":
      $theValue = ($theValue != "") ? "'" . $theValue . "'" : "NULL";
      break;    
    case "long":
    case "int":
      $theValue = ($theValue != "") ? intval($theValue) : "NULL";
      break;
    case "double":
      $theValue = ($theValue != "") ? doubleval($theValue) : "NULL";
      break;
    case "date":
      $theValue = ($theValue != "") ? "'" . $theValue . "'" : "NULL";
      break;
    case "defined":
      $theValue = ($theValue != "") ? $theDefinedValue : $theNotDefinedValue;
      break;
  }
  return $theValue;
}
}

              <h3>How we think about optimism</h3>
              <p>For the purposes of this survey we have defined optimism as a relatively stable<span class="lsua">*</span> disposition some people have that leads them to anticipate positive rather than negative events and outcomes. In this view, pessimism is the opposite of optimism. Individuals can fall anywhere on a pessimism-optimism scale. Our scale runs from  0 – 5. Higher scores reflect a more optimistic disposition than lower scores. </p>
              <h3>Levels of optimism </h3>
              <p><strong><img src="../images/FirmFeet.jpg" alt="feet firmly planted on the ground" width="124" height="90" align="left" class="imageleft" />A very high level of pessimism</strong>—a score lower than 1.25 on our scale—indicates that you tend to be  pessimistic about most things. It is likely that your friends or family  have complained to you about your pessimism, and you probably think that the amount of optimism expressed by others is  ridiculously unrealistic. There may be few people you are willing to trust. </p>
              <p><strong>A moderate level of pessimism</strong>—a score between 1.25 and 2.50—indicates that you tend to be cautiously pessimistic. This is likely to mean that you are more careful than most people when it comes to making decisions or planning for the future, and you may find it difficult to trust people you do not know well. A moderate level of pessimism is likely to be associated with a low level of risk-taking.</p>
              <p><strong>A moderate level of optimism</strong>—a score between 2.50 and 3.75—indicates that you tend to be cautiously optimistic. This could mean that you are a bit less cautious than most people when it comes to making decisions or planning for the future, and you probably find it relatively easy to trust people even before you know them well. A moderate level of optimism is likely to be associated with moderate risk-taking. </p>
              <p><strong><img src="../images/PieInSky.jpg" alt="pie in the sky" width="124" height="90" align="left" class="imageleft" />A very high level of optimism</strong>—a score higher than 3.75 on our scale—indicates that you tend to be optimistic about most things. It is likely that your friends or family  have complained to you about your optimism. You even may have let others down when one or more of your optimism-fueled ventures failed. This is because a very high level of optimism can contribute to a high level of risk-taking, which is associated with both positive and negative outcomes. For example, many successful entrepreneurs  are optimists, but the same can be said of failed entrepreneurs (and other victims of their own high risk behavior).</p>
              <h3>Optimism and experience</h3>
              <p>As we mature, our   optimism (or pessimism) is likely to be tempered by  experience. We may become slower to trust others because we have experienced betrayal, or we may have experienced one or more rewarding relationships, making us more optimistic about future relationships. We may begin to regard the future as less certain due to political or economic change, or positive events like the birth of a child may have lightened our view of the future. Even though our expectations tend to affect the kind of choices we make—and our choices are almost certain to affect our experiences—life still can surprise us by  conflicting with our expectations. When it does, some people change.</p>
              <h3>Correlates of optimism</h3>
              <p>Higher levels of  optimism have been related to things as diverse as entrepreneurship, adjustment to stress, higher socioeconomic status, lower distress, less avoidant behavior, longer survival rates for cancer sufferers, greater immunity, better problem recognition, the propensity to search for solutions, and healthier lifestyle choices. However, these relationships are weak, none of them are causal<span class="lsua"><strong>†</strong></span>, and many of the findings have been contradicted in other research. Moreover, there is very little study of the negative effects of optimism. Norey and Chang, in an August 2002, <em>Journal of Clinical Psychology</em> article, suggested that researchers tend to underestimate or ignore the negative costs of optimism. We agree.</p>
              <h3>Take home message</h3>
              <p>Optimism, like other human qualities, has both positive and negative implications. You can use your survey results to   get  started thinking about the role your level of optimism has played in your life. We suggest that you begin by thinking about the ways in which your own level of optimism has affected your choices, relationships, and experiences. What are the advantage and disadvantages of your level of optimism? Are you satisfied or dissatisfied with your level of optimism? Do you wish you were more or less optimistic? What kind of feedback have you had from others regarding your level of optimism? Try <a href="../OLD/lectical_journal_entry.php">journaling</a> about these issues or discussing them with others. </p>
              <p>&nbsp;</p>
              <p><span class="lsua">*</span>We say &quot;relatively stable&quot; because optimism can be affected by experience. For example, researchers have shown that  stress of various kinds can reduce optimism—at least temporarily. </p>
              <p><strong class="lsua">†</strong>It has not been shown that optimism causes any of these behaviors or outcomes.</p>

              <h3 class="pspaced">Intuitive versus explicit knowledge</h3>
              <p class="pspaced">Sometimes, people know more about a particular subject than they can communicate in an assessment like this one. This is because much of what we learn through experience does not automatically become part of our conscious knowledge. It's in a form that's difficult to share. We call this kind of knowledge &quot;tacit&quot; or &quot;implicit&quot;. You can also think of it as intuitive knowledge. Tacit knowledge is not a bad thing. It helps us make quick choices in familiar situations; we'd be in big trouble if we had to think through every single situation in our lives before we made a decision! But tacit knowledge has its limits. </p>
              <p class="pspaced"><strong>First</strong>, because we aren't able to bring it into focus, we can't share tacit knowledge with others. This is the case in businesses in which the person at the top is an &quot;intuitive&quot; leader. Because his or her leadership skills are tacit, they can't be shared. In situations like this, it is not uncommon for an institution to last only as long as its leader. </p>
              <p class="pspaced"><strong>Second</strong>, it is limited by our experience, which means it isn't terribly useful for dealing with novelty. Because tacit knowledge is experienced-based, it is most useful in situations that are like those we have confronted in the past. Unfortunately, the modern world is constantly providing us with new challenges. To meet these challenges, we  need conscious methods for evaluating  knowledge and experience.</p>
              <p class="pspaced"><strong>Third</strong>, sometimes our tacit knowledge is sub-optimal. For example, people who learn the skills required to survive on the rough side of town or in an abusive relationship often fare poorly when they try to function outside of those contexts, because their  tacit knowledge isn't useful in their new surroundings. Before they can learn more adaptive skills, they usually need to bring their tacit knowledge into consciousness. </p>
              <p class="pspaced"><strong>Finally</strong>, relying on tacit knowledge limits our development. When we habitually rely on our tacit knowledge, learning not only slows down, its quality is compromised. Optimal learning requires that we reflect consciously upon our own experiences and actively seek other perspectives to fill in the gaps.</p>
