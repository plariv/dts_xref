  <div class="AccordionPanelTab">Lectical™ Assessment: A new standard in developmental assessment</div>
              <div class="AccordionPanelContent">
                <p>Since 1993, our founder, Dr. <a href="../about/theodawson.html">Theo Dawson</a>, has been designing, testing, and working with a developmental assessment system called the <a href="../PDF/LAS.pdf" >Lectical™ Assessment System</a> (LAS). The system is based on Harvard Professor <a href="http://www.gse.harvard.edu/~ddl/publication.htm">Kurt Fischer's</a> <em>Dynamic Skill Scale</em>, and can be used to score<span class="emphasis"> the developmental level of any sample of reasoning in any area of knowledge</span>.</p>
                <p>The scale we use is composed of 13 levels, covering the entire life-span. Our  assessments have been designed to capture the top 5 levels of the scale, which are observed in adolescence and adulthood. Junior high school students primarily reason at levels 9 and 10, high school students usually reason at level 10, and college graduates (or individuals with equivalent knowledge and experience) may demonstrate reasoning at level 11 in some areas. A small percentage of adults are able to reason at levels 12 or 13 in one or more disciplines.</p>
                <p>Our assessment system <a href="../about/las_reliability_validity.php">reliably</a>  distinguishes 4  phases per level. This means we can identify 12-13 distinct phases in adults, each of which has specific implications for performance.</p>
                <p>To date, we have completed the research required to design three employee assessments, all for managers—the <a href="../about/assessldma.php">LDMA</a> (managerial decision making), <a href="../about/assessllra.php">LLRA</a> (leadership reasoning),  and <a href="../about/assesslrja.php">LRJA</a> (reflective judgment).</p>

                <p>Our research into the development of leadership reasoning has exposed several more or less distinct knowledge areas, each of which unfolds through a sequence of developmental levels. Interestingly, although it is often desirable for skills in all of these knowledge areas to develop at the same rate, a substantial body of research shows that this is not always the case. In fact, <span class="emphasis">reasoning  skills in different knowledge areas often develop at different rates</span>. For example, a philosophy major may score near the top of our scale on  reflective judgment, but perform no better than the average college sophomore on managerial decision making, whereas a mid-level manager may perform as expected on managerial decision making but lag behind in reflective judgment.</p>
                <p>For this reason,<span class="emphasis"> it is necessary to assess development in the specific areas that are important to you</span>. If you want to know how a mid-level manger approaches managerial problems, you need an assessment of workplace decision-making. If you want to know how she is likely to approach learning, you need an assessment of reflective judgment. If you want to know how she thinks about and values the leadership role, you need an assessment of leadership reasoning. If you want a profile, you need several assessments.</p>

                <p><em>For managers and individuals who are thinking about moving into management</em></p>
                <p>The <a href="../about/assessldma.php">LDMA</a> examines the quality of adults' decision making skills when confronted with real-life leadership problems that require the coordination of different perspectives, values, and power relationships. For example, a dilemma presented in one form of the LDMA requires the test taker to deal with a hostile employee response  to  demands made by a new senior manager. Another dilemma asks the test taker how to deal with an employee whose level of productivity has suddenly decreased during a period of high customer demand. </p>

Moreover, good decision making skills are essential for strategic thinking. If you are going to use one assessment of leadership skills, we encourage you to consider the LDMA.</p>

                <p>Building on the work of <a href="http://gsi.berkeley.edu/textonly/resources/learning/perry.html">Perry</a> and <a href="http://www.umich.edu/~refjudg/reflectivejudgmentmodel.html">Kitchener &amp; King</a>, we have designed a general assessment of student's reflective judgment—the <a href="../about/assesslrja.php">LRJA</a>. This assessment, which requires testees to reason through one of several challenging real-world dilemmas, is composed of a set of essay questions, followed by a short  survey. Level of performance on this assessment provides a general sense of how an individual approaches knowledge and learning. </p>

There is a substantial research literature on the relation between reflective judgment and learning. This literature shows that <span class="emphasis">individuals whose reflective judgment skills are more developed can learn and use more complex and abstract information</span>. The LRJA is especially useful in helping to determine <span class="emphasis">(1)</span> what kind of training will be useful to a particular individual or <span class="emphasis">(2)</span> if it is time to for an individual to tackle the next level of the management ladder.</p>

              <div class="AccordionPanelTab">Lectical scores</div>
              <div class="AccordionPanelContent">
                <p>All assessments are scored by  <strong>Certified Lectical Analysts</strong>, who are required to maintain a high standard of accuracy and reliability (85% agreement within 1/4 of a level with a Master Lectical Analyst). It is important to understand that lectical scores are not like grades or percentile scores, which tell you where individuals are performing relative to others. Lectical scores are more like positions on a ruler or thermometer. Each level and phase is defined in terms of the <em>types</em> of reasoning tasks someone performing at that level can accomplish. This ruler-like quality is unprecedented in academic assessment. It makes it possible for us not only to provide a score, but to tell you (1) <span class="emphasis">how well a manager's skills line up with the task demands of a particular job</span>, or (2) <span class="emphasis">what that manager needs to learn next </span>to  prepare for the demands of that job.</p>
