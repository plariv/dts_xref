        <td valign="top"><h1>ASSESSMENTS FOR EDUCATIONAL CONSULTANTS</h1>
          <h3>Not just another test</h3>
          <p>As an educational consultant, your primary goal is to find the right school for each of the students with whom you work. To accomplish this goal, it's likely that you're  already evaluating the talents and skills of your students with a range of qualitative and quantitative assessments. In fact, you may be wondering why you should ask the students you work with to sit through yet another test. </p>
          <p><span class="emphasis">The answer is simple.</span> Conventional tests can't tell you how well students think, and how well they think is one of the best predictors of how well they will perform in a given academic setting. </p>
          <p>See our <a href="../PDF/LASschools.pdf">brochure</a>.</p>
          <p>&nbsp;</p>
          <div id="Accordion1" class="Accordion">
<div class="AccordionPanel">
  <div class="AccordionPanelTab">Lectical™ Assessment: A new standard in developmental assessment</div>
              <div class="AccordionPanelContent">
                <p>Since 1993, our founder, Theo Dawson, has been designing, testing, and working with a developmental assessment system called the <a href="../PDF/LAS.pdf" >Lectical™ Assessment System</a>. The system is based on Harvard Professor <a href="http://www.gse.harvard.edu/~ddl/publication.htm">Kurt Fischer's</a> <em>Dynamic Skill Scale</em>, and can be used to <span class="emphasis">score the developmental level of any sample of reasoning in any area of knowledge</span>. </p>
                <p>The scale we use is composed of 13 levels, which cover the entire life-span. Our  assessments have been designed to capture the top 5 levels of the scale, which are commonly observed from age 10 onward. Middle and high school students generally demonstrate reasoning at levels 9 and 10 (occasionally 11). Our assessment system reliably  distinguishes 4  phases per level. This means we can identify 8-10 distinct phases in the middle to high school age-group, each of which has specific implications for learning.</p>
                <p>For general information about our assessments, <a href="../about/assessments.php">click here</a>.</p>
                <p>&nbsp;</p>
              </div>
            </div>
            <div class="AccordionPanel">
              <div class="AccordionPanelTab">The lectical level  of a student's reasoning predicts school success</div>
              <div class="AccordionPanelContent">
                <p>Decades of  <a href="../about/tradvsdev.html">research</a> have shown that <span class="emphasis">students with more developed reasoning  skills are more likely to succeed</span> in challenging academic settings. This is because  more challenging curricula require more complex reasoning and the complexity of students' reasoning increases with development. For example, most first year college curricula prepare students with the knowledge and skills they will need to progress to level 11. Students who reason in phases 10:4 and 11:1 are optimally prepared to learn in this kind of environment. In other words, they are likely to learn what their professors require. Students who reason in phases 10:2 and 10:3 are  less likely to learn as required.  Students who are already reasoning above phase 11:1 may be  bored with first year courses that require a great deal of memorization, and end up performing poorly or dropping out.</p>
                <p>&nbsp;</p>
              </div>
            </div>
            <div class="AccordionPanel">
              <div class="AccordionPanelTab">The difference between lectical assessments and other standardized tests</div>
              <div class="AccordionPanelContent">
                <p>Standardized assessments like the SAT are not assessments of reasoning skill. They are assessments of  knowledge.  Lectical assessments of reasoning skill <em>complement</em> assessments of content knowledge, adding another dimension to your understanding of a given student's current capabilities, and helping you to make more informed recommendations. <span class="emphasis">Some students  have high scores on standardized tests and low scores on lectical assessments.</span> These  are often highly intelligent students who have successfully memorized a great deal of content knowledge. Based on grades and test scores, they are frequently admitted into challenging schools, but disappoint their instructors by failing to reason well about what they have learned. <span class="emphasis">Other students  have low scores on standardized tests and high scores on lectical assessments.</span> These students, who are often unusually talented, may not be admitted into the very schools in which they would excel. We hate to think how many talented students fall through this &quot;assessment gap&quot; each year.</p>
                <p>See our <a href="../PDF/LASschools.pdf">brochure</a>.</p>
                <p>&nbsp;</p>
              </div>
            </div>
            <div class="AccordionPanel">
              <div class="AccordionPanelTab">The LRJA (Lectical Reflective Judgment Assessment)</div>
              <div class="AccordionPanelContent">
                <p>Cognitive developmental psychologists have studied how students learn in many knowledge areas, including, math, science, ethics, decision making, art, and the social domain, among others. One area that has undergone extensive study is <em>reflective judgment</em> (a major component of critical thinking). Researchers repeatedly have found that <span class="emphasis">individuals whose reflective judgment skills are more developed can learn and use more complex and abstract information</span>. In fact, level of reflective judgment has been shown to be positively related to learning [<a href="../about/referencesrj.html">1-9</a>], academic performance [<a href="../about/referencesrj.html">5, 8, 10-16</a>], problem-solving and decision making [<a href="../about/referencesrj.html">17-20</a>], tolerance [<a href="../about/referencesrj.html">21</a>], adaptability [<a href="../about/referencesrj.html">22</a>], moral development [<a href="../about/referencesrj.html">23</a>], and ego development [<a href="../about/referencesrj.html">5, 23</a>].</p>
                <p>Building on the work of <a href="http://gsi.berkeley.edu/textonly/resources/learning/perry.html">Perry</a> and <a href="http://www.umich.edu/~refjudg/reflectivejudgmentmodel.html">Kitchener &amp;  King</a>, we have designed a general assessment of student's reflective judgment—the <a href="../about/assesslrja.php">LRJA</a>. This assessment, which requires students to reason through one of several challenging real-world dilemmas, is composed of a set of essay questions, followed by a short preference survey. Level of performance on this assessment provides a general sense of how an individual reasons and how he or she is  likely to approach information and learning.</p>
                <p>&nbsp;</p>
              </div>

            <div class="AccordionPanel">
              <div class="AccordionPanelTab">Lectical scores</div>
              <div class="AccordionPanelContent">
                <p>All assessments are scored by  Certified Lectical Analysts, who are required to maintain a high standard of accuracy and <a href="../about/las_reliability_validity.php">reliability</a> (85% agreement within 1/4 of a level with a Master Lectical Analyst). </p>
                <p>It is important to understand that lectical scores are not like grades or percentile scores, which tell you where a student performs relative to other students. Lectical scores are more like <a href="../about/measurement.php">positions on a ruler or thermometer</a>. Each level and phase is defined in terms of the reasoning tasks someone performing at that level can accomplish. This ruler-like quality is unprecedented in academic assessment. It makes it possible for us not only to provide a score, but to tell you what that score means for a student's learning.</p>
                <p class="emphasis"> We can tell you what each student who takes one of our assessments needs to learn next to make optimal progress <em>and</em> what level they need to reason at to succeed in a given educational program.</p>
