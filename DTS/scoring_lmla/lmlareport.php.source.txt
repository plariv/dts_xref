function GetSQLValueString($theValue, $theType, $theDefinedValue = "", $theNotDefinedValue = "") 
{
  if (PHP_VERSION < 6) {
    $theValue = get_magic_quotes_gpc() ? stripslashes($theValue) : $theValue;
  }

  $theValue = function_exists("mysql_real_escape_string") ? mysql_real_escape_string($theValue) : mysql_escape_string($theValue);

  switch ($theType) {
    case "text":
      $theValue = ($theValue != "") ? "'" . $theValue . "'" : "NULL";
      break;    
    case "long":
    case "int":
      $theValue = ($theValue != "") ? intval($theValue) : "NULL";
      break;
    case "double":
      $theValue = ($theValue != "") ? doubleval($theValue) : "NULL";
      break;
    case "date":
      $theValue = ($theValue != "") ? "'" . $theValue . "'" : "NULL";
      break;
    case "defined":
      $theValue = ($theValue != "") ? $theDefinedValue : $theNotDefinedValue;
      break;
  }
  return $theValue;
}
}

                      <h3 class="pspaced">The rate of development</h3>
                      <p class="pspaced">As Malcolm Gladwell argues in the book, <em>Outliers</em>, it takes about 10,000 hours of  study (10 years) to become an expert in any complex fieldno matter how talented you are. We would add that no matter how much of an expert you becomeon yourself, in your area of professional expertise, or in the interpersonal spherethere is always room for growth. </p>

<h3 class="pspaced">Task demands and human limitations</h3>
                    <p class="pspaced">The task demands of the highest levels of management/leadership in most large (and many smaller) institutions are far beyond the reach of individual leaders. At these levels, leaders must work with others to identify and integrate a wide range of knowledge, information, and perspectives. In these cases, the performance level of the group can be a full level higher than the performance levels of the individuals who make up the group.                    </p>
                    <h3 align="left"><span>Implicit versus explicit knowledge</span></h3>
                    <p align="left"><span>Some of our knowlege is readily avaialble to us. We are conscious of it and can share it with others. Psychologists refer to this kind of knowledge as <em>explicit</em>. Explicit knowledge is fairly easy to share on an assessment like this one. However, people often know more about a particular subject than they can communicate in an assessment like this one. This is because much of what we learn through experience does not automatically become part of our explicit knowledge. It's in a form that's difficult to share. We call this kind of knowledge &quot;tacit&quot; or &quot;implicit&quot;. You can also think of it as intuitive knowledge. Tacit knowledge is not a bad thing. It helps us make quick choices in familiar situations; we'd be in big trouble if we had to think through every single situation in our lives before we made a decision! But tacit knowledge has its limits. </span></p>
                    <p class="pspaced"><strong>First</strong>, because we aren't able to bring it into focus, we can't share tacit knowledge with others. This is the case in businesses in which the person at the top is an &quot;intuitive&quot; leader. Because his or her leadership skills are tacit, they can't be shared. In situations like this, it is not uncommon for an institution to last only as long as its leader. </p>
                    <p class="pspaced"><strong>Second</strong>, it is limited by our experience, which means it isn't terribly useful for dealing with novelty. Because tacit knowledge is experienced-based, it is most useful in situations that are like those we have confronted in the past. Unfortunately, the modern world is constantly providing us with new challenges. To meet these challenges, we  need conscious methods for evaluating  knowledge and experience.</p>
                    <p class="pspaced"><strong>Third</strong>, sometimes our tacit knowledge is sub-optimal. For example, people who learn the skills required to survive on the rough side of town or in an abusive relationship often fare poorly when they try to function outside of those contexts, because their  tacit knowledge isn't useful in their new surroundings. Before they can learn more adaptive skills, they usually need to bring their tacit knowledge into consciousness. </p>
                    <p><span class="pspaced"><strong>Fourth</strong>, relying on tacit knowledge limits our development. When we habitually rely on our tacit knowledge, learning not only slows down, it's quality is compromised. Optimal learning requires that we reflect consciously upon our own experiences and actively seek other perspectives to</span> fill in the gaps.                    </p>
                    <p><strong>Finally</strong>, relying upon tacit knowledge limits our ability to collaborate with others. </p>
                    <h3><strong>Our learning recomme<span class="pspaced">ndations</span></strong></h3>
                    <p class="pspaced">As Malcolm Gladwell argues in the book, <em>Outliers</em>, it takes about 10,000 hours of constant study (10 years) to become an expert in any complex fieldno matter how talented you are. We would add that no matter how much of an expert you becomeon yourself, in your area of professional expertise, or in the interpersonal spherethere is always room for growth. </p>
                    <p class="pspaced">Our  learning suggestions are targeted one to two phases beyond your current phase of performance. They should make you stretch just enough to challenge your current thinking. If a resource is too easy, you should probably move on. If it is too difficult, start with something a little less challenging.</p>
                    <p><span class="pspaced">As you work with the resources suggested on the following tabs, keep in mind that deep learning requires practice. You are far more likely to incorporate new knowledge into your skill repertoire if you take every opportunity to (1) put it to work, and (2) reflect upon outcomes. Putting knowledge to work means applying it in real-world contexts. Try out new skills in low-stakes situations first, then gradually begin to apply them in higher-stakes situations until you've mastered them. Reflecting on outcomes means evaluating  the results of  your efforts. This works best if you keep a recordsuch as a journaland share your experiences with other members of your community of practice. If you are a senior manager or CEO, you may want to think about how you</span>r corporate culture supports or prevents this kind of discourse.                    </p>
